\title{A semi-Analytic Framework for Long-Short Position Sizing in DeFi under Liquidation and Jump Risk}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study the initial sizing of a leveraged DeFi long--short position whose solvency is
governed by a protocol health factor. The position is modelled as absorbed when the health
factor crosses the liquidation boundary; practical liquidation mechanics are outside the
model scope. Under bivariate Kou double-exponential jump--diffusion dynamics, we reduce
surviving-path payoff moments to one-dimensional killed expectations of the log
collateral-to-liability ratio. An exponential change of measure absorbs the short-leg
price factor, while the exponential jump structure reduces the Laplace-domain resolvent to
a finite-dimensional linear system. Crucially, the construction is available for every
admissible integer moment order $k\ge1$ satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$: changing $k$ changes
the tilt and forcing term, but not the dimension of the $3\times3$ linear system. The
framework therefore provides fast semi-analytic estimates of survival probabilities and
conditional payoff moments of arbitrary admissible integer order as functions of the initial
log health buffer, adapting classical first-passage machinery to the DeFi health-factor payoff
setting.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
DeFi lending; leveraged long--short; health factor; Kou double-exponential jump diffusion; first-passage problem; Laplace resolvent; optimal portfolio sizing
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

DeFi lending protocols such as AAVE and Compound enable a leveraged long--short strategy: deposit a
cryptocurrency as collateral, borrow a second asset, and sell it short. The position is governed by a
real-time \emph{health factor} — a borrowing-power-adjusted ratio of collateral to liability — and is
liquidated automatically and irrevocably the instant that ratio falls below one. A practitioner must
choose the initial coin holdings to balance two opposing forces: a more aggressive position amplifies
expected returns on surviving paths but raises the probability of forced exit. Cryptocurrency markets
compound the difficulty — daily log-returns of 10--20\% are common and flash crashes can exceed 50\%
— making fat-tailed jump dynamics essential for realistic liquidation probabilities. 

This paper provides the first exact, semi-analytic solution to the optimal sizing problem under a
\emph{survival-conditioned solvent-path payoff model}: the objective is defined on the payoff
realised on paths that avoid liquidation, and the liquidation boundary is treated as an absorbing
barrier.

We work under the Kou double-exponential jump--diffusion framework \citep{Kou2002}. The solution
rests on two successive dimensional reductions. First, an exponential change of measure absorbs the
short-leg price factor into the path-measure density, collapsing what is nominally a bivariate barrier
problem to a \emph{univariate} first-exit problem for the log-ratio process under a tilted
L\'{e}vy measure. Second, because exponential jump sizes are eigenfunctions of the Kou generator,
the Laplace-domain resolvent of the first-exit problem reduces to a $3\!\times\!3$ linear system —
the same size for every admissible integer moment order $k$ — that is solved in closed form.
Thus the method is not restricted to the first two moments used by mean--variance sizing:
it computes each integer-order raw or conditional moment for which $k\eta_{2,+}<1$. Time-domain quantities
follow by numerical Laplace inversion via the Talbot contour algorithm \citep{Talbot1979}. The entire
optimisation therefore reduces to a scalar search over a single parameter, the \emph{initial log
health factor}, with the conditional expected payoff, its variance, and the liquidation probability
all expressible as semi-analytic functions of that scalar. Unlike Monte Carlo, which suffers
from sampling noise that becomes severe when few paths survive liquidation and requires fresh
simulation at each candidate $h_0$, the semi-analytic expressions are noise-free and
differentiable in $h_0$, reducing the optimization to a smooth scalar search; numerical
experiments confirm a ${\approx}55\times$ speed-up over Monte Carlo.

This paper sits at the intersection of the DeFi liquidation literature and the analytical
first-passage literature for jump diffusions.
\citet{Qin2021} and \citet{HeimbachHuang2024} document the incentive structure and leverage
amplification of DeFi liquidations; these papers are empirical and do not address the ex-ante optimal
sizing problem. Filling that gap requires tools from the first-passage literature for jump diffusions:
\citet{KouWang2003} derive explicit Laplace-transform results for first-passage times under
double-exponential jump diffusion, and \citet{Sepp2004} extends this to single- and double-barrier
option pricing, reducing the resolvent to a finite algebraic system — the same structural advantage
exploited here. The difference is that the payoff is a barrier-killed long--short ratio, not a
single-asset barrier option; the change-of-measure reduction is the key step that bridges the two
settings. More distantly, \citet{JacobsLevyStarer1999} advocate joint optimisation of long and short
legs, while \citet{DangForsyth2016} study dynamic mean--variance control under jump risk; our setting
is static and the binding constraint is an endogenous first-passage event rather than a variance bound.

The paper makes three contributions to the sizing of leveraged DeFi positions.
First, it establishes the initial log health factor $h_0$ as the natural scalar sizing
variable: once a unit-initial-value constraint is imposed, the two-dimensional portfolio
problem collapses to a scalar health-buffer decision, with the raw killed moments
$\E[\Pi_T^k]$ for every admissible integer order $k\ge1$ satisfying
$k\eta_{2,+}<1$, and the liquidation
probability $p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0)$, all explicit in $h_0$ alone.
Second, it delivers those quantities semi-analytically — noise-free, differentiable in
$h_0$, and ${\approx}55\times$ faster than Monte Carlo. For each admissible integer
$k$, the calculation requires a new tilt and Laplace inversion but retains the same
$3\times3$ resolvent system; its algebraic dimension does not grow with $k$. This makes
gradient-based optimisation of any criterion of the form
$\Phi\!\left(\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T],\ldots,\E[\Pi_T^K\mid\tau>T],p_{\mathrm{liq}}\right)$
practical for any finite admissible integer $K$.
Third, it characterises how the optimal buffer trades conditional upside against
liquidation risk, providing a principled alternative to the ad-hoc collateral ratios
used in practice.
The framework abstracts away liquidation execution, dynamic collateral management,
stochastic borrow rates, and oracle delays; it is a liquidation-buffer evaluation engine
for ex-ante position sizing, not a complete trading system.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:setup} introduces the position,
health factor, payoff, and the one-dimensional reduction to a scalar optimisation.
Section~\ref{sec:model} specifies the asset dynamics under the Kou model;
Section~\ref{sec:tilted} introduces the tilted-measure machinery.
Section~\ref{sec:moments} shows how the change of measure decouples the short-leg factor from the
barrier problem, reducing each raw killed moment $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ to a one-dimensional killed
expectation for every integer $k\ge1$ satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$; dividing by
$p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$ yields
the conditional moments entering the general objective \eqref{eq:Phi-general}. Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} derives the respective resolvents.
Section~\ref{sec:numerical} presents numerical results, comparing the Laplace--resolvent method
against Monte Carlo simulation across a range of initial log health factor values and verifying the
analytical no-shorting limit.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} concludes and discusses the practical interpretation of the results.
Appendix~\ref{sec:calibration} describes the empirical characteristic function estimator used to
calibrate the bivariate Kou parameters from observed bivariate log-returns.
Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} collects the proofs.

\section{Problem Setup}\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================

We consider a leveraged long--short portfolio in which an investor holds a long position in asset $S_1$
and a short position in asset $S_2$, financed so that the net initial value of the book equals one unit of
account. The strategy is subject to a risk-management rule that forces liquidation if the long leg falls
sufficiently relative to the short leg, reflecting margin requirements or stop-loss constraints common in
practice. The objective is to choose the initial coin holdings so as to optimise a performance criterion
defined on the terminal payoff, conditional on surviving the liquidation threshold.

\subsection{Surviving-path terminal wealth}
Let $(\Omega,\F,(\F_t)_{t\ge 0},\P)$ be a filtered probability space.
Two strictly positive asset prices $S_1,S_2$ evolve as
\[
S_{i,t}=S_{i,0}e^{X_{i,t}},\qquad X_{i,0}=0,\quad i=1,2,
\]
where $X_1,X_2$ are log-price processes to be specified in Section~\ref{sec:model}.
The investor holds $n_1>0$ units of asset $S_1$ (long leg) and $n_2>0$ units of asset $S_2$ (short leg).

Let
\[
V_{1,t}:=n_1 S_{1,t},\qquad V_{2,t}:=n_2 S_{2,t}
\]
denote the \emph{collateral value} (long leg) and the \emph{borrow value} (outstanding liability) at
time $t$. Fix $b\in(0,1)$, the \emph{liquidation threshold} of asset $S_1$ (the Aave v3 parameter of the
same name; distinct from the maximum LTV at origination, which is a separate and typically lower
protocol constant): the protocol liquidates the position the first time the borrow value
exceeds the $b$-adjusted collateral,
\[
b\,V_{1,t} \;\ge\; V_{2,t}.
\]
The \emph{health factor} summarises this margin condition as a single ratio,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:health-factor}
H_t := \frac{b\,V_{1,t}}{V_{2,t}} = \frac{b\,n_1 S_{1,t}}{n_2 S_{2,t}},
\qquad
h_t := \log H_t,
\end{equation}
where $h_t$ is the \emph{log health factor}. The solvency condition is $H_t\ge 1$
($h_t\ge 0$); breaching it is equivalent to the borrow value exceeding the $b$-adjusted collateral.
Liquidation is triggered at the first such instant:
\[
\tau:=\inf\{t\ge 0:\;H_t<1\} = \inf\{t\ge 0:\;h_t<0\}.
\]
A larger $b$ permits a larger borrow-to-collateral ratio $V_2/V_1\le b$ before liquidation,
giving the position more room to absorb adverse price moves; a smaller $b$ is more conservative,
triggering earlier forced exit and limiting the protocol's risk of bad debt.

Since
\[
h_t
=
\log\!\Big(\frac{b\,n_1S_{1,0}}{n_2S_{2,0}}\Big)+(X_{1,t}-X_{2,t}),
\]
define the initial log health factor and the log-ratio process by
\[
h_0:=\log\!\Big(\frac{b\,n_1S_{1,0}}{n_2S_{2,0}}\Big),
\qquad
Z_t:=X_{1,t}-X_{2,t},
\]
so that $h_t=h_0+Z_t$ and the liquidation time is equivalently
\[
\tau=\inf\{t\ge 0:\; Z_t<-h_0\}.
\]
The one-dimensional process $Z$ thus inherits all the relevant risk: the position survives if and only if
$Z$ stays above the absorbing level $-h_0$. The parameter $h_0 = \log H_0$ is therefore the
\emph{initial log-cushion} — the log-distance of the initial health factor from the liquidation
boundary $H=1$. A larger $h_0$ means the position starts further from forced exit; $h_0\to 0^+$
means the position starts almost at the boundary with negligible cushion.

The \emph{surviving-path terminal wealth} at horizon $T$ is
\[
\Pi_T:=(n_1S_{1,T}-n_2S_{2,T})\,\one_{\{\tau>T\}}.
\]
On $\{\tau>T\}$ the health factor satisfies $H_T\ge 1$, so $b\,n_1S_{1,T}\ge n_2S_{2,T}$;
since $b\in(0,1)$ this forces $n_1S_{1,T}>n_2S_{2,T}$, meaning the position carries strictly
positive terminal equity on every surviving path. No positive-part truncation is needed.
The indicator $\one_{\{\tau>T\}}$ assigns zero terminal wealth to liquidated paths; the remark
below explains why zero is used as a conservative proxy for the residual recovery value.
Since the initial net book value is normalised to one unit of account (Section~\ref{sec:1d}),
net profit on a surviving path is $R_T:=\Pi_T-1$, but all moments in this paper are stated in
terms of $\Pi_T$ directly.
This paper characterises the distribution of $\Pi_T$ on the event $\{\tau>T\}$ and the
probability of that event, with the boundary $H_t=1$ treated as an absorbing state; the
liquidation mechanics are outside the model scope.

\begin{remark}[Liquidation mechanics]\label{rem:liquidation}
When $H_t$ hits one, a DeFi lending protocol does not simply extinguish the position: it
initiates a protocol-specific liquidation procedure executed by third-party keepers.
In AAVE v3, a keeper may close up to 50\% of the debt (100\% if the health factor falls below a
secondary threshold), claiming a \emph{liquidation bonus} of 5--15\% of the seized collateral as
compensation; Compound uses a fixed close factor with a similar bonus structure
\citep{Qin2021,HeimbachHuang2024}.

The execution price received by the keeper — and hence the residual recovery value for the
borrower — is itself uncertain. Liquidations in DeFi are settled on-chain via decentralised
exchanges: in stressed markets, the available liquidity may be insufficient to absorb a large
collateral sale without significant slippage, compressing the borrower's recovery further
\citep{Qin2021}. The mechanics therefore depend on the specific protocol version, the pool
liquidity at the moment of liquidation, the gas price environment, and the speed of keeper
response — none of which are captured by a single-barrier first-passage model.

For these reasons we assign zero payoff to liquidated paths: $\Pi_T := 0$ on $\{\tau\le T\}$.
This is a \emph{conservative proxy} — it understates the expected residual value whenever the
borrower retains a fractional recovery, and overstates the loss relative to protocols that
execute partial liquidations. Its practical justification is two-fold. First, the borrower
typically has no control over the execution details once the boundary is breached; from an
ex-ante sizing perspective the liquidation payoff is a zero-mean random variable with uncertain
sign, and setting it to zero is the risk-neutral midpoint. Second, the penalty term
$\delta\,p_{\mathrm{liq}}$ in the objective of \eqref{eq:Phi} provides a separate lever to
price liquidation risk at whatever level the investor deems appropriate, without requiring an
explicit model of the post-liquidation cash flow. Assigning zero to liquidated paths is
therefore \emph{conservative} — it never overstates residual value — and \emph{practical}:
it avoids an unverifiable recovery model while keeping the payoff specification tractable.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Dynamic collateral management]\label{rem:dynamic}
A practitioner who monitors the health factor in continuous time could top up collateral
or partially unwind the short leg to steer $H_t$ away from the liquidation boundary.
Such a strategy would replace the absorbing barrier with a reflecting or controlled
boundary and the static sizing problem with a stochastic control problem.
We do not pursue this extension for two reasons.
First, on-chain collateral top-ups incur gas costs and are subject to block-confirmation
delays; during a flash crash the position may be liquidated before a remedial transaction
is confirmed, so the absorbing-barrier model represents the practically relevant worst case.
Second, the paper's central question is the ex-ante buffer decision — how much initial
cushion to hold given that dynamic intervention may be unavailable — and a static
framework isolates that question cleanly.
\end{remark}

Taken together, the two modelling choices — zero recovery at liquidation and no dynamic
intervention — define a \emph{conservative floor} for position sizing: any position that
is viable under these assumptions remains viable whenever protocol outcomes are more
favourable.

Using the definition of $H_T$, one obtains the following factorization.

\begin{lemma}[Payoff factorization]\label{lem:payoff-factor}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:payoff-factor}
\Pi_T
=
\frac{n_2}{b}\,S_{2,0}e^{X_{2,T}}\,(e^{h_0+Z_T}-b)\,\one_{\{\tau>T\}}.
\end{equation}
The factor $(e^{h_0+Z_T}-b)$ is strictly positive on $\{\tau>T\}$ because $H_T=e^{h_0+Z_T}\ge 1>b$,
consistent with the definition of $\Pi_T$.
\end{lemma}
This separates the payoff into a numeraire factor driven by $S_2$, and a barrier-killed option on the
log-ratio $Z$ with moneyness controlled by $h_0$.

\subsection{Optimization setup}\label{sec:1d}

The two coin holdings $(n_1,n_2)$ represent two degrees of freedom. A normalisation reduces this to a single
scalar, and the structure of the payoff then identifies $h_0$ as the natural optimisation variable.

We impose the unit-initial-value constraint
\[
n_1S_{1,0}-n_2S_{2,0}=1,
\]
which fixes the net book value at inception. Setting $x:=n_2S_{2,0}$, one has $n_1S_{1,0}=1+x$ and
$e^{h_0}=b(1+x)/x$. Solving for $x$,
\[
x=\frac{b}{e^{h_0}-b},
\]
which yields the holding map
\begin{equation}\label{eq:n-from-h0}
n_2(h_0)=\frac{b}{S_{2,0}(e^{h_0}-b)},
\qquad
n_1(h_0)=\frac{e^{h_0}}{S_{1,0}(e^{h_0}-b)}.
\end{equation}
Positivity of both holdings requires $e^{h_0}>b$, i.e.
\[
h_0>\ln b.
\]
The initial gross leverage — long-leg value per unit of equity — follows directly from
\eqref{eq:n-from-h0}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:leverage}
L_0 := n_1(h_0)\,S_{1,0} = \frac{e^{h_0}}{e^{h_0}-b}.
\end{equation}
$L_0$ is strictly decreasing in $h_0$: as $h_0\downarrow\ln b$, leverage $L_0\uparrow\infty$;
as $h_0\to\infty$, leverage $L_0\downarrow 1$ (the short leg vanishes and the position becomes
unlevered). Equivalently, the borrow-to-equity ratio is $L_0 - 1 = b/(e^{h_0}-b)$, which also
collapses the initial health factor back to $H_0=e^{h_0}$ via $H_0 = b\,L_0/(L_0-1)$.
Choosing $h_0$ is therefore equivalent to choosing the initial leverage.

Since every quantity of interest is determined by $h_0$ through \eqref{eq:n-from-h0} and the
killed process $(Z,\tau)$, the portfolio problem reduces to a scalar optimisation
\[
\max_{h_0\,\in\,(\ln b,\,\infty)}\;\Phi\!\left(h_0\right).
\]
The optimal holdings are recovered by substituting $h_0^\star$ back into \eqref{eq:n-from-h0};
we call $h_0^\star$ the \emph{optimal initial health buffer}.

The framework supports any performance criterion of the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi-general}
\Phi(h_0) \;=\; f\!\left(\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T],\,\ldots,\,\E[\Pi_T^K\mid\tau>T],\;p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\E[\Pi_T^k\mid\tau>T]=\E[\Pi_T^k]/p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$ are the conditional moments
obtained from the raw killed moments $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ delivered in Section~\ref{sec:moments},
$p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0):=1-p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$, and $f$ is any smooth function.
The admissible range of moment orders is $k=1,\ldots,K$ with
$K\eta_{2,+}<1$, a bound determined by the integrability of $e^{kX_{2,T}}$ under the
exponential tilt (Proposition~\ref{prop:integer-moments}).
Here $K$ is not fixed by the method: any positive integer $K$ satisfying the integrability
bound may be selected, and the same construction is applied separately at each
$k\in\{1,\ldots,K\}$.
This class covers the classical mean--variance criterion \citep{Markowitz1952}, which uses
$\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T]$ and $\E[\Pi_T^2\mid\tau>T]$; higher-moment objectives that penalise skewness or excess kurtosis, which
use $\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T],\ldots,\E[\Pi_T^4\mid\tau>T]$; the conditional Sharpe ratio; and polynomial approximations to
expected utility of arbitrary order.
\citet{DangForsyth2016} and \citet{ShenZou2022} adopt mean--variance criteria in related
jump-diffusion settings; the distinguishing feature here is that $p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0)$ is
an explicit, separately computed building block rather than an implicit risk charge.

For the numerical illustration in Section~\ref{sec:numerical} we adopt the penalised
conditional mean--variance criterion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phi}
\Phi(h_0)
:=
\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T]
-
\tfrac{\alpha}{2}\,\mathrm{Var}(\Pi_T\mid\tau>T)
-
\delta\,p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha\ge 0$ is a risk-aversion coefficient and $\delta\ge 0$ penalises forced
liquidation. The optimal $h_0^\star$ balances leverage against downside protection: a lower
$h_0$ implies higher leverage and a larger conditional upside but a higher liquidation
probability; a higher $h_0$ provides more cushion at the cost of a smaller conditional
payoff. We do not refer to $h_0^\star$ as ``optimal leverage'' because leverage is a derived
quantity \eqref{eq:leverage}; the investor chooses the health buffer and leverage follows.

The remainder of the paper is devoted to computing the building blocks of \eqref{eq:Phi-general}:
the raw killed moments $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ for every admissible integer $k\ge1$ satisfying
$k\eta_{2,+}<1$, and the
survival probability $p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$, each as an explicit semi-analytic function
of $h_0$ via the Laplace--resolvent method of Section~\ref{sec:resolvent}.
The conditional moments $\E[\Pi_T^k\mid\tau>T]$ and liquidation probability $p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0)$
then follow immediately.

\section{Asset Dynamics}\label{sec:model}
% ============================================================
\subsection{Bivariate Kou model}
We assume under $\P$ that each log-price satisfies the SDE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:log-price-sde}
\diff X_{i,t}
=
\mu_i^X\,\diff t
+
\sigma_i\,\diff B_{i,t}
+
\diff\!\left(\sum_{n=1}^{N_{i,t}} J_i^{(n)}\right),
\qquad i=1,2,
\end{equation}
where $B_1,B_2$ are correlated standard Brownian motions with $\diff\langle B_1,B_2\rangle_t=\rho\,\diff t$,
$N_i$ is a Poisson process with intensity $\lambda_i$ independent of $(B_1,B_2)$, and the jump sizes
$J_i^{(n)}$ are i.i.d.\ with the Kou double-exponential law.
The jump density, characteristic function, and moment generating function are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kou-char-mgf}
f_i(j)
=
\frac{p_i}{\eta_{i,+}}e^{-j/\eta_{i,+}}\one_{\{j>0\}}
+
\frac{1-p_i}{\eta_{i,-}}e^{j/\eta_{i,-}}\one_{\{j<0\}},
\qquad
\varphi_{J_i}(u)
=
\frac{p_i}{1-iu\eta_{i,+}}
+
\frac{1-p_i}{1+iu\eta_{i,-}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MGF}
M_i(s)
=
\frac{p_i}{1-s\eta_{i,+}}
+
\frac{1-p_i}{1+s\eta_{i,-}},
\qquad
s\in\left(-\tfrac{1}{\eta_{i,-}},\,\tfrac{1}{\eta_{i,+}}\right).
\end{equation}
Here $\eta_{i,+}$ and $\eta_{i,-}$ are the \emph{means} of the positive and negative jump sizes
(not exponential rates); the corresponding rates are $1/\eta_{i,+}$ and $1/\eta_{i,-}$.
The constraint $\eta_{i,+}<1$ is required for the price-jump compensator $\chi_i$ defined below to be finite.

The parameter $\mu_i$ is the \emph{continuously compounded expected asset-price growth rate},
i.e.\ $\E_\P[S_{i,t}/S_{i,0}]=e^{\mu_i t}$.
Define the price-jump compensator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chi}
\chi_i
:=
M_i(1)-1
=
\E[e^{J_i}-1]
=
\frac{p_i}{1-\eta_{i,+}}+\frac{1-p_i}{1+\eta_{i,-}}-1.
\end{equation}
Since $\E_\P[e^{X_{i,t}}]=\exp(t[\mu_i^X+\tfrac12\sigma_i^2+\lambda_i\chi_i])$,
matching to $e^{\mu_i t}$ gives the log-price drift coefficient
\begin{equation}\label{eq:muX-drift}
\mu_i^X := \mu_i - \tfrac12\sigma_i^2 - \lambda_i\chi_i.
\end{equation}
All analytical and numerical objects in the sequel use $\mu_i^X$.

The bivariate process $(X_1,X_2)$ is a L\'evy process with joint characteristic exponent
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Psi}
\begin{aligned}
\Psi(u,v)
&:= i(\mu_1^X u+\mu_2^X v)
-\tfrac12\Big(\sigma_1^2u^2+\sigma_2^2v^2+2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2uv\Big)\\
&\quad +\lambda_1\big(\varphi_{J_1}(u)-1\big)
+\lambda_2\big(\varphi_{J_2}(v)-1\big),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
so that $\E_\P[e^{i(uX_{1,t}+vX_{2,t})}]=e^{t\Psi(u,v)}$.

\subsection{Tilted Dynamics}\label{sec:tilted}
% ============================================================

For each integer $k\ge 0$, define the $k$-tilted measure $\P^{(k)}$ by
\[
\left.\frac{\diff\P^{(k)}}{\diff\P}\right|_{\F_t}
=
\frac{e^{kX_{2,t}}}{\E_{\P}[e^{kX_{2,t}}]}
=
\exp\!\left(kX_{2,t}-t\,\Psi(0,-ik)\right),
\qquad
k\eta_{2,+}<1.
\]
\begin{lemma}[Lévy exponent under $\P^{(k)}$]\label{lem:psiZk}
Under $\P^{(k)}$, the log-ratio $Z=X_1-X_2$ is again a Lévy process with exponent
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psiZk}
\psi_Z^{(k)}(s)
=
\mu_Z^{(k)}s+\tfrac12\sigma_Z^2 s^2
+\lambda_1\big(M_1(s)-1\big)
+\lambda_2\big(M_2(k-s)-M_2(k)\big),
\end{equation}
where
\[
\sigma_Z^2:=\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2-2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2\ge 0,
\qquad
\mu_Z^{(k)}:=(\mu_1^X-\mu_2^X)-k(\sigma_2^2-\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2).
\]
In particular, the generator satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generator-eigen}
\mathcal{L}^{(k)}e^{sz}=\psi_Z^{(k)}(s)\,e^{sz}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\proofref{proof:lem:psiZk}

This eigenrelation is the key identity used in Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} to build the resolvent equation.

\begin{remark}[Superscript $(k)$ convention]\label{rem:k-notation}
A superscript $(k)$ indicates evaluation under $\P^{(k)}$. The untilted case $k=0$ recovers the
original dynamics of $Z$ under $\P$. For $k\ge 1$, the $k$-th moment $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ is computed
under $\P^{(k)}$; the constraint $k\eta_{2,+}<1$ ensures the tilted measure and the characteristic
root structure remain well-defined. Throughout, $k$ is an integer. There is no restriction
to $k=1$ or $k=2$: every positive integer satisfying the integrability condition is covered.
\end{remark}

For the Kou structure, the tilt transforms the jump rates of $Z$ into the $k$-dependent phase rates
\[
r_{1,+}^{(k)}:=\frac{1}{\eta_{1,+}},
\quad
r_{1,-}^{(k)}:=\frac{1}{\eta_{1,-}},
\quad
r_{2,+}^{(k)}:=\frac{1}{\eta_{2,-}}+k,
\quad
r_{2,-}^{(k)}:=\frac{1}{\eta_{2,+}}-k.
\]
Because $Z=X_1-X_2$, positive jumps of $X_2$ appear as downward jumps of $Z$: the tilt by $e^{kX_2}$
changes the downward phase rate from $1/\eta_{2,+}$ to $1/\eta_{2,+}-k$, which is positive precisely
when $k\eta_{2,+}<1$.
\begin{definition}[Phase operators]\label{def:phase-ops}
For $j=1,2$, the \emph{phase operators} associated with the tilted jump kernels of $Z$ under $\P^{(k)}$ are
\[
K_{j,+}^{(k)}[f](z)
:=
\int_0^\infty f(z+y)\,r_{j,+}^{(k)}e^{-r_{j,+}^{(k)}y}\,\diff y,
\qquad
K_{j,-}^{(k)}[f](z)
:=
\int_{-\infty}^0 f(z+y)\,r_{j,-}^{(k)}e^{r_{j,-}^{(k)}y}\,\diff y.
\]
This construction originates in \citet{KouWang2003} for first-passage problems under the
double-exponential model, and is used by \citet{Sepp2004} to reduce double-barrier pricing
to a finite linear system.
For any $\gamma$ with $\gamma < r_{j,+}^{(k)}$ and $\gamma > -r_{j,-}^{(k)}$, evaluating
the integrals directly gives the eigenfunction identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phase-on-exp}
K_{j,+}^{(k)}[e^{\gamma\cdot}](z)
=
\frac{r_{j,+}^{(k)}}{r_{j,+}^{(k)}-\gamma}\,e^{\gamma z},
\qquad
K_{j,-}^{(k)}[e^{\gamma\cdot}](z)
=
\frac{r_{j,-}^{(k)}}{r_{j,-}^{(k)}+\gamma}\,e^{\gamma z}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}
Each phase operator maps an exponential to a scalar multiple of the same exponential, which is
the key algebraic identity exploited in Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} to convert the resolvent
PIDE into a finite-dimensional linear system.

\section{Moments via Measure Change}\label{sec:moments}
% ============================================================

Fix any integer $k\ge1$ satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$. Computing
$\E_\P[\Pi_T^k]$ directly under $\P$ is intractable: the payoff couples the joint
process $(X_1,X_2)$ to the barrier event $\{\tau>T\}$ in a way that is neither separable nor
analytically tractable. An exponential change of measure resolves this by tilting the path measure by
$e^{kX_{2,T}}$, which absorbs the short-leg factor into the density and leaves a purely one-dimensional
killed expectation for the log-ratio $Z=X_1-X_2$ under the $k$-tilted measure $\P^{(k)}$. Since $Z$
remains a L\'{e}vy process under $\P^{(k)}$, no information about the joint dynamics is lost: the
tilted characteristic exponent encodes the full marginal law of $Z$. The $k$-th moment then factorises
as the known scalar $e^{T\Psi(0,-ik)}=\E_\P[e^{kX_{2,T}}]$ times the barrier-killed expectation
$m_k(T;h_0)$ under $\P^{(k)}$, which is computed via the Laplace--resolvent of
Section~\ref{sec:resolvent}.

The argument below is order-generic. It is applied independently at each desired integer
$k$, and the resulting resolvent always has a $3\times3$ barrier system. Higher integer
orders enlarge only the finite forcing sum from $j=0,\ldots,k$; they do not enlarge the
linear system.

Define
\[
g_k(z;h_0):=(e^{h_0+z}-b)^k.
\]
Since the survival event $\{\tau>T\}$ implies $Z_T>-h_0$, we have $e^{h_0+Z_T}>1>b$ on $\{\tau>T\}$, so $g_k(Z_T;h_0)=(e^{h_0+Z_T}-b)^k>0$ and is a polynomial in $e^{Z_T}$.
From \eqref{eq:payoff-factor},
\[
\Pi_T^k
=
\Big(\frac{n_2}{b}\Big)^k S_{2,0}^k e^{kX_{2,T}}\,g_k(Z_T;h_0)\,\one_{\{\tau>T\}}.
\]
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moment-reduction}
\E_{\P}[\Pi_T^k]
=
\Big(\frac{n_2}{b}\Big)^k S_{2,0}^k\,e^{T\Psi(0,-ik)}\;
\E_{\P^{(k)}}\!\Big[g_k(Z_T;h_0)\,\one_{\{\tau>T\}}\Big].
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Every admissible integer-order moment]\label{prop:integer-moments}
Let $k\ge 1$ be an integer satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$. Define
\[
m_k(T;h_0):=\E_{\P^{(k)}}\!\Big[g_k(Z_T;h_0)\,\one_{\{\tau>T\}}\Big].
\]
Then
\[
\E[\Pi_T^k]:=\E_{\P}[\Pi_T^k]=\frac{e^{T\Psi(0,-ik)}}{(e^{h_0}-b)^k}\,m_k(T;h_0),
\]
where $m_k(T;h_0)=U^{(k)}(T,0;h_0)$ is computed via the Laplace--resolvent method of
Section~\ref{sec:resolvent}. Consequently, the method computes $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ for every
integer
\[
k\in\mathcal K:=\{n\in\mathbb N:\ n\eta_{2,+}<1\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

Let $p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0):=\P(\tau>T)$ and $p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0):=1-p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$.
Since $\Pi_T$ already contains the survival indicator $\one_{\{\tau>T\}}$, the conditional $k$-th
moment on surviving paths is
\[
\E[\Pi_T^k\mid\tau>T]
=
\frac{\E[\Pi_T^k]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)},
\qquad k\ge 1,\quad k\eta_{2,+}<1.
\]
In particular,
\[
\E[\Pi_T\mid\tau>T]
=
\frac{\E[\Pi_T]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)},
\qquad
\mathrm{Var}(\Pi_T\mid\tau>T)
=
\frac{\E[\Pi_T^2]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)}
-
\left(\frac{\E[\Pi_T]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)}\right)^{\!2}.
\]
The mean--variance--liquidation objective is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:objective}
\Phi(h_0)
=
\frac{\E[\Pi_T]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)}
-
\frac{\alpha}{2}
\!\left[
\frac{\E[\Pi_T^2]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)}
-
\!\left(\frac{\E[\Pi_T]}{p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)}\right)^{\!2}
\right]
-
\delta\,p_{\mathrm{liq}}(h_0),
\end{equation}
and the portfolio optimization reduces to the one-dimensional problem
\[
\max_{h_0\in(\ln b,\infty)}\Phi(h_0),
\]
after which the optimal coin holdings are recovered from \eqref{eq:n-from-h0}.

\begin{remark}[No-shorting limit]\label{rem:no-shorting}
As $h_0\to\infty$ the holding map gives $n_2 S_{2,0}\to 0$ and $n_1 S_{1,0}\to 1$, so liquidation
becomes impossible and the portfolio converges to a unit long position in asset~$1$. For every $k\ge 1$,
\[
\lim_{h_0\to\infty}\E[\Pi_T^k]=\E[e^{kX_{1,T}}]=e^{T\Psi(-ik,0)},
\]
which provides a useful diagnostic: the formula for $\E[\Pi_T^k]$ must satisfy this limit exactly.
The $k=1$ case gives $e^{\mu_1 T}$ by the drift normalisation \eqref{eq:muX-drift}.
\end{remark}

The remainder of the analysis is devoted to computing $m_k(T;h_0)$ for every admissible integer $k\ge 1$
and $p_{\mathrm{surv}}(h_0)$ via the Laplace--resolvent construction.

% ============================================================
\section{Resolvent Equations for Moments and Survival Probability}\label{sec:resolvent}
% ============================================================

Proofs of all results in Sections~\ref{sec:tilted}--\ref{sec:resolvent} are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.

With the moment problem reduced to a one-dimensional killed expectation under $\P^{(k)}$, the
function $U(t,z)=\E_z^{(k)}[g_k(Z_t;h_0)\one_{\{\tau>t\}}]$ satisfies a PIDE in $(t,z)$.
Taking the Laplace transform in $t$ converts this to a stationary integro-differential equation in
$z$ alone, with the Laplace parameter acting as a discount rate. The Kou structure then reduces
it further: because exponentials are eigenfunctions of the generator, every integro-differential
term acts algebraically on an exponential ansatz, and the resolvent collapses to a finite-dimensional
linear system. Three boundary conditions at the absorbing barrier close the $3\times 3$ system.
Importantly, this system remains $3\times3$ for every admissible integer $k$; increasing the
moment order changes its coefficients and the forcing polynomial, not its dimension.
The survival probability follows by the same architecture at zero tilt ($k=0$). This section derives
both resolvents in closed form.

\subsection{Killed Moment Resolvent}\label{sec:moment-resolvent}

Fix an integer $k\ge 1$ with $k\eta_{2,+}<1$. Define the killed moment function
\[
U(t,z) := \E_z^{(k)}\!\left[g_k(Z_t;h_0)\,\one_{\{\tau(h_0)>t\}}\right],
\qquad z > -h_0,
\]
with $U(t,z)=0$ for $z\le -h_0$. This satisfies the PIDE
\[
\partial_t U = \mathcal{L}^{(k)} U, \qquad z > -h_0,
\]
with initial condition $U(0,z) = g_k(z;h_0)$. Define the Laplace transform
\[
\widehat{U}(q,z) := \int_0^\infty e^{-qt}U(t,z)\,\diff t, \qquad q > 0.
\]
Applying it to both sides of the PIDE proceeds in two steps.

\textit{Left-hand side.} Integrate by parts in $t$:
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-qt}\,\partial_t U(t,z)\,\diff t
=
\Bigl[e^{-qt}U(t,z)\Bigr]_0^\infty
+
q\int_0^\infty e^{-qt}U(t,z)\,\diff t
=
-U(0,z) + q\widehat{U}(q,z),
\]
where the boundary term at $t=\infty$ vanishes by integrability.

\textit{Right-hand side.} Since $\mathcal{L}^{(k)}$ acts only in $z$, it commutes with the $t$-integral:
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-qt}\,\mathcal{L}^{(k)}U(t,z)\,\diff t
=
\mathcal{L}^{(k)}\widehat{U}(q,z).
\]

Equating and substituting the initial condition $U(0,z)=g_k(z;h_0)$ gives the resolvent equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:resolvent}
(q - \mathcal{L}^{(k)})\widehat{U}(q,z) = g_k(z;h_0), \qquad z > -h_0,
\end{equation}
with $\widehat{U}(q,z)=0$ for $z\le -h_0$.

\begin{assumption}[Generic six-root regime]\label{ass:six-root}
Fix an integer $k\ge 0$ satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$, and let $q>0$. Assume:
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item $\sigma_Z>0$;
\item the characteristic equation $\psi_Z^{(k)}(\gamma)=q$ has six simple roots $\gamma_1(q),\dots,\gamma_6(q)$,
ordered so that
\[
\Rea(\gamma_{1,2,3})>0,\qquad \Rea(\gamma_{4,5,6})<0;
\]
\item $q\neq \psi_Z^{(k)}(j)$ for $j=0,\dots,k$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}

Since $b\in(0,1)$ and $z>-h_0$ together imply $e^{h_0+z}>1>b$, the payoff kernel expands as a polynomial in $e^z$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:forcing-expansion}
g_k(z;h_0) = \sum_{j=0}^k c_{k,j}(h_0)\,e^{jz},
\qquad
c_{k,j}(h_0) := \binom{k}{j}(-1)^{k-j}b^{k-j}e^{jh_0}.
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Particular solution]\label{lem:Upart}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(iii),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Upart}
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,z;h_0) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q - \psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}\,e^{jz}
\end{equation}
is a particular solution to~\eqref{eq:resolvent}.
\end{lemma}
\proofref{proof:lem:Upart}

\begin{lemma}[Homogeneous solution]\label{lem:Uhom}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(i)--(ii), the general solution to $(q-\mathcal{L}^{(k)})u=0$ on $z>-h_0$ that
is admissible at $+\infty$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Uhom}
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}(q,z;h_0) = \sum_{m=1}^{3} B_m^{(k)}(q;h_0)\,e^{\gamma_{m+3}(q)(z+h_0)},
\end{equation}
where $B_1^{(k)},B_2^{(k)},B_3^{(k)}$ are free constants.
\end{lemma}
\proofref{proof:lem:Uhom}

The general solution on $z>-h_0$ is then
\[
\widehat{U}(q,z;h_0) = \widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,z;h_0) + \widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}(q,z;h_0).
\]

\begin{proposition}[Barrier resolvent]\label{prop:Ubar}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root} and the nondegeneracy condition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nondeg-bar}
r_{1,-}^{(k)}\neq r_{2,-}^{(k)},
\end{equation}
the boundary conditions at $z=-h_0^+$ uniquely determine
$\mathbf{B}^{(k)}(q;h_0):=(B_1^{(k)},B_2^{(k)},B_3^{(k)})^\top$ via the linear system
\[
\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)\,\mathbf{B}^{(k)}(q;h_0)=-\mathbf{b}^{(k)}(q;h_0),
\]
where the $3\times 3$ barrier matrix is
\[
\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 1\\[6pt]
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(k)}}{r_{1,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_4(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(k)}}{r_{1,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_5(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(k)}}{r_{1,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_6(q)}\\[12pt]
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(k)}}{r_{2,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_4(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(k)}}{r_{2,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_5(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(k)}}{r_{2,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_6(q)}
\end{pmatrix}
\]
and the forcing vector is
\[
\mathbf{b}^{(k)}(q;h_0)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,-h_0;h_0)\\[4pt]
K_{1,-}^{(k)}\!\left[\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,\cdot\,;h_0)\right](-h_0)\\[4pt]
K_{2,-}^{(k)}\!\left[\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,\cdot\,;h_0)\right](-h_0)
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^{k}\frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q-\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}\,e^{-jh_0}\\[10pt]
\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^{k}\frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q-\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}\,\frac{r_{1,-}^{(k)}}{r_{1,-}^{(k)}+j}\,e^{-jh_0}\\[10pt]
\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^{k}\frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q-\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}\,\frac{r_{2,-}^{(k)}}{r_{2,-}^{(k)}+j}\,e^{-jh_0}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:Mbar-inv} below, $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)$ is invertible, giving
\[
\mathbf{B}^{(k)}(q;h_0) = -\bigl(\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)\bigr)^{-1}\mathbf{b}^{(k)}(q;h_0).
\]
\end{proposition}
\proofref{proof:prop:Ubar}

\begin{lemma}[Invertibility of $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)$]\label{lem:Mbar-inv}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root} and condition~\eqref{eq:nondeg-bar},
the barrier matrix $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)$ of Proposition~\ref{prop:Ubar} is invertible.
\end{lemma}
\proofref{proof:lem:Mbar-inv}

Evaluating $\widehat{U}$ at the initial state $Z_0=0$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Uhat-zero}
\widehat{U}(q,0;h_0)
=
\sum_{j=0}^{k}\frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q-\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}
+
\sum_{m=1}^{3}B_m^{(k)}(q;h_0)\,e^{\gamma_{m+3}(q)h_0}.
\end{equation}
The killed moment is then recovered by Laplace inversion:
\[
m_k(T;h_0) = \mathcal{L}^{-1}_{q\to T}\!\left[\widehat{U}(q,0;h_0)\right].
\]

\subsection{Survival Probability}\label{sec:liquidation}

The same resolvent architecture yields the survival probability. Define
\[
p_{\mathrm{surv}}(t,z;h_0):=\P_z(\tau(h_0)>t),
\qquad
p_{\mathrm{liq}}(t,z;h_0):=1-p_{\mathrm{surv}}(t,z;h_0).
\]
Let $\mathcal L^{(0)}$ denote the generator of $Z$ under the untilted measure, with exponent $\psi_Z^{(0)}$, and define
\[
\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,z;h_0)
:=
\int_0^\infty e^{-qt}p_{\mathrm{surv}}(t,z;h_0)\,\diff t.
\]

The problem is
\[
(q-\mathcal L^{(0)})\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,z;h_0)=1,
\qquad z>-h_0,
\]
with
\[
\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,z;h_0)=0,\qquad z\le -h_0.
\]
The domain is the half-line $(-h_0,\infty)$, the boundary is absorbing at $z=-h_0$, and there is no interface because
the source is constant.

\begin{proposition}[Survival resolvent and liquidation probability]\label{prop:survival}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root} and the nondegeneracy condition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nondeg-surv}
r_{1,-}^{(0)}\neq r_{2,-}^{(0)},
\quad\text{i.e.,}\quad
\eta_{1,-}\neq\eta_{2,+},
\end{equation}
let $\gamma_4^{(0)}(q),\gamma_5^{(0)}(q),\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)$ be the three roots of
$\psi_Z^{(0)}(\gamma)=q$ with negative real part. Then
\[
\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,z;h_0)
=
\frac{1}{q}
+
C_1(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_4^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)}
+
C_2(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_5^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)}
+
C_3(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)},
\]
where the coefficients $\mathbf{C}(q;h_0):=(C_1,C_2,C_3)^\top$ satisfy the linear system
\[
\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)\,\mathbf{C}(q;h_0)
=
-\begin{pmatrix}1/q\\1/q\\1/q\end{pmatrix},
\]
with the $3\times 3$ survival matrix
\[
\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 1\\[6pt]
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(0)}}{r_{1,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_4^{(0)}(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(0)}}{r_{1,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_5^{(0)}(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{1,-}^{(0)}}{r_{1,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)}\\[12pt]
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(0)}}{r_{2,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_4^{(0)}(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(0)}}{r_{2,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_5^{(0)}(q)} &
\dfrac{r_{2,-}^{(0)}}{r_{2,-}^{(0)}+\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:Msurv-inv} below, $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)$ is invertible, giving
\[
\mathbf{C}(q;h_0) = -\bigl(\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)\bigr)^{-1}\begin{pmatrix}1/q\\1/q\\1/q\end{pmatrix}.
\]
At the initial state $Z_0=0$,
\[
\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,0;h_0)
=
\frac{1}{q}
+
C_1(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_4^{(0)}(q)h_0}
+
C_2(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_5^{(0)}(q)h_0}
+
C_3(q;h_0)e^{\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)h_0}.
\]
Hence
\[
p_{\mathrm{surv}}(T,0;h_0)
=
\mathcal L^{-1}_{q\to T}\!\left[\widehat U_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,0;h_0)\right],
\qquad
p_{\mathrm{liq}}(T,0;h_0)=1-p_{\mathrm{surv}}(T,0;h_0).
\]
\end{proposition}
\proofref{proof:prop:survival}

\begin{lemma}[Invertibility of $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)$]\label{lem:Msurv-inv}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root} and condition~\eqref{eq:nondeg-surv},
the survival matrix $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)$ of Proposition~\ref{prop:survival} is invertible.
\end{lemma}
\proofref{proof:lem:Msurv-inv}

$k=0,1,2,3,4$ and verify the analytical no-shorting limit as $h_0\to\infty$.
These orders illustrate rather than limit the method: the same implementation applies to
every admissible integer $k$, which under the calibrated parameters includes $k=1,\ldots,79$.
Time-domain quantities are recovered via the Talbot deformed-contour algorithm

\section{Conclusion and Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

This paper develops a semi-analytic method for sizing a leveraged DeFi long--short position
subject to health-factor liquidation. The initial log health factor $h_0$ reduces the sizing
problem to one dimension, while an exponential change of measure reduces each payoff moment
to a killed expectation of the log-ratio process. Under Kou jump--diffusion dynamics, the
corresponding Laplace resolvent is obtained from a $3\times3$ linear system.

A central feature is that the construction applies to every admissible integer order
$k\ge1$ satisfying $k\eta_{2,+}<1$. Higher-order moments require a different tilt and forcing
polynomial, but do not increase the dimension of the resolvent system. The framework therefore
supports mean--variance sizing as well as criteria involving skewness, kurtosis, higher-moment
penalties, or polynomial utility approximations. The numerical results show close agreement
with Monte Carlo, while the semi-analytic method is substantially faster and free of simulation
noise.

For practical use, an investor can first choose a maximum liquidation probability and invert
$p_{\mathrm{liq}}(H_0)$ to obtain a minimum health factor. Within the resulting feasible set,
the investor then maximises the preferred moment-based objective. The optimal leverage follows
from the selected health factor through $L_0=H_0/(H_0-b)$. Figure~\ref{fig:frontier}
summarises this tradeoff between conditional payoff, variance, and liquidation probability.

The model deliberately abstracts from oracle delays, liquidation execution and recovery,
dynamic collateral adjustments, and stochastic borrowing rates. It should therefore be read
as an ex-ante liquidation-buffer and sizing tool rather than a complete trading or execution
model.

Further work may incorporate liquidation recovery, dynamic collateral management, and
stochastic funding costs.

\section{Empirical Calibration}\label{sec:calibration}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Estimator}\label{sec:ecf-estimator}

The bivariate Kou model admits the closed-form Lévy--Khintchine exponent
$\Psi(u,v)$ derived in Section~\ref{sec:model}, so no numerical density
inversion is required to compute the model-implied characteristic function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:model-cf}
\phi_\theta(u,v;\Delta)
=
\exp\!\bigl(\Delta\,\Psi_\theta(u,v)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta$ is the observation interval.
Given $N$ observed bivariate log-return pairs $(r_{1,n},r_{2,n})_{n=1}^N$,
the empirical characteristic function is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ecf}
\widehat{\phi}_N(u,v)
=
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N
\exp\!\bigl(i(u\,r_{1,n}+v\,r_{2,n})\bigr).
\end{equation}
We estimate $\theta$ by minimising the weighted squared distance between
\eqref{eq:ecf} and \eqref{eq:model-cf} over a discrete frequency grid $\mathcal{G}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ecf-objective}
\widehat{\theta}
=
\arg\min_{\theta\in\Theta}\,
Q_N(\theta),
\qquad
Q_N(\theta)
=
\sum_{(u,v)\in\mathcal{G}}
w(u,v)
\,\bigl|\widehat{\phi}_N(u,v)-\phi_\theta(u,v;\Delta)\bigr|^2.
\end{equation}
This is the empirical-characteristic-function approach of
\citet{Singleton2001}, which is particularly natural for affine and
Lévy-based asset-pricing models whose characteristic functions are known in
closed form.
\citet{FeuervergerMcDunnough1981} establish that ECF estimators over a
rich grid can attain maximum-likelihood efficiency.
The procedure avoids evaluating the bivariate transition density, which would
require a two-dimensional numerical Fourier inversion inside every optimizer
call.
For background on DEJD likelihood estimation in the univariate case
see \citet{RamezaniZeng2007}.

\subsection{Frequency grid}\label{sec:ecf-grid}

The parameter vector $\theta$ has $13$ free components; see Table~\ref{tab:params}.
Identification is most efficient when the frequency grid $\mathcal{G}$
probes the directions that are informative for different parameter groups.
We use three groups:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{G}_1$ \emph{(marginal directions)}:
    $\{(u,0):u\in\mathcal{U}\}\cup\{(0,v):v\in\mathcal{U}\}$.
    These directions identify the marginal Kou parameters of each asset
    independently.
  \item $\mathcal{G}_2$ \emph{(joint directions)}:
    $\{(u,v):u,v\in\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{joint}}\}$.
    Cross-frequency pairs identify the Brownian correlation $\rho$.
  \item $\mathcal{G}_Z$ \emph{(spread direction)}:
    $\{(s,-s):s\in\mathcal{U}_Z\}$.
    Along this direction $u\,r_1+v\,r_2=s(r_1-r_2)$, so the
    estimator directly matches the characteristic function of the
    log-ratio process $Z=X_1-X_2$ that governs DeFi liquidation.
    Including $\mathcal{G}_Z$ explicitly ties the calibration to the
    object that drives barrier crossing.
\end{itemize}
The full grid is $\mathcal{G}=\mathcal{G}_1\cup\mathcal{G}_2\cup\mathcal{G}_Z$.
Default frequency nodes are
$\mathcal{U}=\{0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0,1.5,2.0,3.0,4.0,5.0\}$,
$\mathcal{U}_{\mathrm{joint}}=\{0.5,1.0,2.0\}$,
$\mathcal{U}_Z=\{0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0,1.5,2.0,3.0\}$,
giving $M=34$ moment conditions for $13$ parameters.
Weights use Gaussian damping
$w(u,v)=\exp(-a(u^2+v^2))$ with $a=1$,
which down-weights high frequencies sensitive to microstructure noise.

\subsection{Reparameterisation and initialisation}\label{sec:ecf-init}

\paragraph{Unconstrained reparameterisation.}
Constraints in Table~\ref{tab:params} are enforced automatically via the
change of variables
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reparam}
\sigma_i=e^{a_i},\quad
\lambda_i=e^{b_i},\quad
p_i=\mathrm{sigmoid}(c_i),\quad
\eta_{i,+}=\mathrm{sigmoid}(d_i),\quad
\eta_{i,-}=e^{e_i},\quad
\rho=\tanh(f),
\end{equation}
where sigmoid$(x)=(1+e^{-x})^{-1}$.
The unconstrained vector $\tau=({\mu_1,a_1,b_1,c_1,d_1,e_1,\mu_2,\ldots,f})$
is optimized with box bounds in this transformed space.  For asset~2 the code uses
a scaled sigmoid,
\[
\eta_{2,+}=\eta_{2,+}^{\max}\mathrm{sigmoid}(d_2),
\qquad
\eta_{2,+}^{\max}=(1-\varepsilon)/K,
\]
so that the moment-admissibility condition $K\eta_{2,+}<1$ is enforced directly.
For asset~1, the sigmoid bound $\eta_{1,+}<1$ keeps the price-jump compensator finite.

\paragraph{Threshold initialiser.}
The characteristic-function objective is non-linear and non-convex,
so the optimisation is initialised by a threshold-based decomposition
following the logic of \citet{Mancini2009} for discretely observed
jump-diffusions.
Let $m_i=\mathrm{median}(r_{i,n})$ and let $s_i$ be the IQR robust scale.
For a frequency-aware threshold constant $c\in[3,5]$, define candidate
jump observations
\[
\mathcal{J}_i=\{n:|r_{i,n}-m_i|>c\,s_i\},
\qquad
\mathcal{C}_i=\{1,\ldots,N\}\setminus\mathcal{J}_i.
\]
Initial volatility and correlation values use the continuous subset
$\mathcal{C}_i$, while the jump-size means are anchored by peak-over-threshold
excess means and the intensities are corrected by a variance/fourth-cumulant
estimate.  Schematically:
\begin{align*}
p_i^{(0)}&=\frac{|\mathcal{J}_{i,+}|}{|\mathcal{J}_i|}
  \quad\text{clamped to the admissible probability range},\\
\eta_{i,+}^{(0)},\,\eta_{i,-}^{(0)}&=\text{POT mean-excess estimates},\\
\lambda_i^{(0)}&=\text{two-moment estimate from variance and fourth cumulant},\\
\sigma_i^{(0)}&=\mathrm{sd}(r_{i,n}:\,n\in\mathcal{C}_i)/\sqrt{\Delta},
&
\rho^{(0)}&=\mathrm{corr}(r_1,r_2)_{n\in\mathcal{C}_1\cap\mathcal{C}_2}.
\end{align*}
The initializer is stored in the same drift convention as the final result:
$\mu_i^{(0)}$ is an annualized expected price-growth drift.  It is initialized
from the sample log-return drift with a damped mean-jump correction,
\[
\mu_i^{(0)}=\bar{r}_i/\Delta
-0.25\,\lambda_i^{(0)}
\left[p_i^{(0)}\eta_{i,+}^{(0)}-(1-p_i^{(0)})\eta_{i,-}^{(0)}\right],
\]
and the corresponding log-process coefficient $\mu_i^{X,(0)}$
is obtained from \eqref{eq:muX-drift}.
The threshold filter is not the final estimator.
It is a warm start that places the optimizer in a sensible region of
parameter space; see the remark in \citet{Mancini2009}.

\paragraph{Multi-start cloud.}
Because $Q_N(\tau)$ may have local minima, the optimizer is run from a
cloud of $n_{\mathrm{start}}$ starting points (default 20).
Each point perturbs the threshold initialiser by applying a multiplicative
factor $\exp(U)$, $U\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(-0.4,0.4)$, to each scale
parameter, and an additive shift $\epsilon\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(-0.15,0.15)$
to $p_i$ and $\rho$.
The estimate returned is the unconstrained L-BFGS-B minimiser with the
lowest objective value across all starts.

\subsection{Identifiability and model comparison}\label{sec:ecf-ident}

The ECF objective $Q_N(\theta)$ is non-convex.
Jump-diffusion models possess approximate ridges along directions that
preserve the total jump variance
$\lambda_i(p_i\eta_{i,+}^2+(1-p_i)\eta_{i,-}^2)$
\citep{RamezaniZeng2007}.
The diffusion volatilities $\sigma_i$ and the Brownian correlation $\rho$
are well-identified from short samples; jump-size and intensity parameters
carry larger finite-sample uncertainty unless the observation window
contains many jump events.
For hourly cryptocurrency data the expected number of jump observations
per trading year is $\lambda_i\times 8760$, which is typically in the
hundreds; daily data with $\lambda_i=2$ yields only $\approx 16$
identifiable jumps per year.

For model comparison, evaluate $Q_N(\widehat\theta)$ across nested models
(GBM, Merton, bivariate Kou) together with liquidation-relevant diagnostics:
empirical versus model $Z$-tail quantiles, empirical versus model
barrier-crossing frequencies, and simulated versus observed drawdowns of $Z$.
These are more directly informative for the sizing application than
standard likelihood-based criteria.

The implementation is in \texttt{optimal\_long\_short/calibration.py};
the demo script \texttt{jobs/calibrate\_kou.py} shows parameter
recovery on synthetic data and produces Figure~\ref{fig:ecf-fit}.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:psiZk}]\label{proof:lem:psiZk}
\textit{Step 1: Laplace transform under $\P^{(k)}$.}
Because $Z_t = X_{1,t}-X_{2,t}$ and the density on $\mathcal{F}_t$ equals
$e^{kX_{2,t}-t\Psi(0,-ik)}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mgf-tilted}
\E_{\P^{(k)}}\!\left[e^{sZ_t}\right]
=
e^{-t\Psi(0,-ik)}\,
\E_\P\!\left[e^{s X_{1,t}+(k-s)X_{2,t}}\right]
=
e^{t\left[\Psi(-is,-i(k-s))-\Psi(0,-ik)\right]},
\end{equation}
where we used $\E_\P[e^{\alpha X_{1,t}+\beta X_{2,t}}]=e^{t\Psi(-i\alpha,-i\beta)}$.
Since the Radon--Nikod\'ym density is exponential-linear in $(X_{1,t},X_{2,t})$, Girsanov's theorem
for L\'evy processes guarantees that $Z$ remains a L\'evy process under $\P^{(k)}$ with exponent
\begin{equation}\label{eq:psiZk-raw}
\psi_Z^{(k)}(s) = \Psi\!\left(-is,\,-i(k-s)\right)-\Psi(0,-ik).
\end{equation}

\textit{Step 2: Expand and simplify.}
Substituting \eqref{eq:Psi} with $(u,v)=(-is,-i(k-s))$, and noting
$u^2=-s^2$, $v^2=-(k-s)^2$, $uv=-s(k-s)$, and $\varphi_{J_i}(-i\cdot)=M_i(\cdot)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Psi-expanded}
\begin{aligned}
\Psi(-is,-i(k-s))
={}&\mu_1^X s + \mu_2^X(k-s)
+\tfrac12\sigma_1^2 s^2+\tfrac12\sigma_2^2(k-s)^2+\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 s(k-s)\\
&+\lambda_1(M_1(s)-1)+\lambda_2(M_2(k-s)-1).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Setting $s=0$ in \eqref{eq:Psi-expanded} gives
$\Psi(0,-ik)=\mu_2^X k + \tfrac12\sigma_2^2 k^2 + \lambda_2(M_2(k)-1)$.
Subtracting and collecting terms by type:

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Drift.}
$\mu_1^X s + \mu_2^X(k-s) - \mu_2^X k
= (\mu_1^X-\mu_2^X)s$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Diffusion.}
Expanding $(k-s)^2 = k^2-2ks+s^2$ and $(k-s) = k-s$:
\begin{align*}
&\tfrac12\sigma_1^2 s^2+\tfrac12\sigma_2^2(k-s)^2+\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 s(k-s)
-\tfrac12\sigma_2^2 k^2\\
&\quad =\tfrac12(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2-2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2)s^2
-k(\sigma_2^2-\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2)s
= \tfrac12\sigma_Z^2 s^2 - k(\sigma_2^2-\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2)s.
\end{align*}

\smallskip
\noindent\textit{Jumps.}
$\lambda_1(M_1(s)-1)+\lambda_2(M_2(k-s)-1)-\lambda_2(M_2(k)-1)
=\lambda_1(M_1(s)-1)+\lambda_2(M_2(k-s)-M_2(k))$.

\smallskip
Combining, the drift coefficient is
$\mu_Z^{(k)}:=(\mu_1^X-\mu_2^X)-k(\sigma_2^2-\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2)$,
which establishes \eqref{eq:psiZk}.
The eigenrelation \eqref{eq:generator-eigen} follows because for any L\'evy process with
exponent $\psi$, the generator satisfies $\mathcal{L} e^{sz}=\psi(s)\,e^{sz}$, as can be
seen by differentiating $\E[e^{sZ_t}]=e^{t\psi(s)}$ with respect to $t$ at $t=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:Upart}]\label{proof:lem:Upart}
Substitute the forcing expansion into the right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:resolvent}:
\[
(q - \mathcal{L}^{(k)})\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}
= \sum_{j=0}^k c_{k,j}(h_0)\,e^{jz}.
\]
Since the sum is finite and $\mathcal{L}^{(k)}$ is linear, it suffices to find, for each $j$, a function $v_j$
satisfying
\[
(q - \mathcal{L}^{(k)})v_j(z) = c_{k,j}(h_0)\,e^{jz}.
\]
Propose the exponential ansatz $v_j(z) = A_j\,e^{jz}$. By the eigenrelation~\eqref{eq:generator-eigen},
\[
\mathcal{L}^{(k)}[A_j e^{jz}] = A_j\,\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)\,e^{jz},
\]
so
\[
(q - \mathcal{L}^{(k)})[A_j e^{jz}]
= A_j\bigl(q - \psi_Z^{(k)}(j)\bigr)e^{jz}.
\]
Matching to $c_{k,j}(h_0)\,e^{jz}$ and using Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(iii) to ensure
$q - \psi_Z^{(k)}(j)\neq 0$ gives
\[
A_j = \frac{c_{k,j}(h_0)}{q - \psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}.
\]
Summing over $j=0,\dots,k$ yields~\eqref{eq:Upart}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:Uhom}]\label{proof:lem:Uhom}
\textit{Step 1: exponential ansatz.} Seek solutions of the form $u(z)=e^{\gamma z}$. By the
eigenrelation~\eqref{eq:generator-eigen},
\[
\mathcal{L}^{(k)}e^{\gamma z} = \psi_Z^{(k)}(\gamma)\,e^{\gamma z},
\]
so $(q-\mathcal{L}^{(k)})e^{\gamma z} = (q - \psi_Z^{(k)}(\gamma))e^{\gamma z}$. This vanishes if and only if
$\gamma$ satisfies the characteristic equation
\[
\psi_Z^{(k)}(\gamma) = q.
\]

\textit{Step 2: root count.} The exponent $\psi_Z^{(k)}$ is the sum of a quadratic term
$\frac{1}{2}\sigma_Z^2\gamma^2$ (non-degenerate by Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(i)) and rational terms from the
Kou jumps. Clearing denominators yields a degree-six polynomial in $\gamma$, so there are exactly six roots
$\gamma_1,\dots,\gamma_6$ (counted with multiplicity). Under Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(ii) all six are simple
and split as $\Rea(\gamma_{1,2,3})>0$ and $\Rea(\gamma_{4,5,6})<0$.

\textit{Step 3: admissibility.} On the half-line $z>-h_0$, the solution must remain bounded as $z\to+\infty$.
Since $e^{\gamma z}\to\infty$ whenever $\Rea(\gamma)>0$, the roots $\gamma_1,\gamma_2,\gamma_3$ are inadmissible.
Only $\gamma_4,\gamma_5,\gamma_6$ are retained.

\textit{Step 4: centering and general solution.} Writing $e^{\gamma_{m+3}z} = e^{\gamma_{m+3}(z+h_0)}\cdot
e^{-\gamma_{m+3}h_0}$ and absorbing the constant factor into the free coefficient, the general admissible
homogeneous solution is
\[
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}(q,z;h_0) = \sum_{m=1}^3 B_m^{(k)}(q;h_0)\,e^{\gamma_{m+3}(q)(z+h_0)},
\]
where $B_1^{(k)},B_2^{(k)},B_3^{(k)}$ are free constants to be determined by the boundary conditions.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:Ubar}]\label{proof:prop:Ubar}
\textit{Step 1: boundary conditions from killing.}
The killing condition $\widehat{U}(q,z;h_0)=0$ for $z\le-h_0$ imposes, at $z=-h_0^+$,
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item $\widehat{U}(q,-h_0^+;h_0)=0$;
\item $K_{j,-}^{(k)}\!\left[\widehat{U}(q,\cdot\,;h_0)\right](-h_0^+)=0$, \quad $j=1,2$.
\end{enumerate}
Condition (i) is continuity at the barrier. Conditions (ii) arise because
$K_{j,-}^{(k)}[f](z)=\int_{-\infty}^0 f(z+y)\,r_{j,-}^{(k)}e^{r_{j,-}^{(k)}y}\diff y$
integrates $f$ over landing points $z+y < z$; for $z=-h_0^+$ every landing point satisfies
$z+y<-h_0$, where $\widehat{U}=0$, so the integral vanishes.

\textit{Step 2: substitute the full solution.}
Write $\widehat{U}=\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}+\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}$ and insert into each condition.
Since the conditions are linear, each splits as
\[
[\text{condition on }\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}] = -[\text{condition on }\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}].
\]

\textit{Step 3: condition (i).}
Evaluating at $z=-h_0$ and using $e^{\gamma_{m+3}(q)\cdot 0}=1$,
\[
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}(q,-h_0;h_0) = \sum_{m=1}^3 B_m^{(k)} = -\,\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,-h_0;h_0),
\]
which is the first row of the system.

\textit{Step 4: conditions (ii).}
Apply $K_{j,-}^{(k)}$ to $\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{hom}}$. By the phase-operator formula~\eqref{eq:phase-on-exp},
\[
K_{j,-}^{(k)}\!\left[e^{\gamma_{m+3}(\cdot+h_0)}\right](-h_0)
=
\frac{r_{j,-}^{(k)}}{r_{j,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_{m+3}(q)}\,e^{\gamma_{m+3}(q)\cdot 0}
=
\frac{r_{j,-}^{(k)}}{r_{j,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_{m+3}(q)},
\]
giving row $j+1$ of the system:
\[
\sum_{m=1}^3 \frac{r_{j,-}^{(k)}}{r_{j,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_{m+3}(q)}\,B_m^{(k)}
=
-K_{j,-}^{(k)}\!\left[\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,\cdot\,;h_0)\right](-h_0).
\]
Applying~\eqref{eq:phase-on-exp} to $\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{part}}(q,z;h_0)=\sum_j\frac{c_{k,j}}{q-\psi_Z^{(k)}(j)}e^{jz}$
yields the explicit entries of $\mathbf{b}^{(k)}$ stated above.

\textit{Step 5: matrix form and solution.}
The three conditions assemble into
$\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)\,\mathbf{B}^{(k)}=-\mathbf{b}^{(k)}$.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:Mbar-inv}, $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)$ is invertible, so
\[
\mathbf{B}^{(k)}(q;h_0)=-\big(\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}(q)\big)^{-1}\mathbf{b}^{(k)}(q;h_0).\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:Mbar-inv}]\label{proof:lem:Mbar-inv}
Subtracting row~1 from rows~2 and~3 replaces entry $(i,m)$ for $i=2,3$ by
$-\gamma_{m+3}(q)/(r_{i-1,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_{m+3}(q))$.
Pulling out $-1$ from each of these two rows (net sign $(-1)^2=1$) and then factoring
$\gamma_{m+3}(q)$ from each column gives
\[
\det\!\bigl(\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{bar}}^{(k)}\bigr)
=
\gamma_4\gamma_5\gamma_6\;
\det\!\left(\frac{1}{x_i+\gamma_{j+3}(q)}\right)_{\!1\le i,j\le 3},
\]
where $x_1:=0$, $x_2:=r_{1,-}^{(k)}$, $x_3:=r_{2,-}^{(k)}$.
The right-hand factor is a Cauchy determinant; the Cauchy formula yields
\[
\det\!\left(\frac{1}{x_i+\gamma_{j+3}}\right)
=
\frac{\prod_{1\le i<i'\le 3}(x_{i'}-x_i)\cdot
      \prod_{4\le m<m'\le 6}(\gamma_{m'}-\gamma_m)}
     {\prod_{i=1}^3\prod_{m=4}^6(x_i+\gamma_m)}.
\]
Since $x_1=0$, the factor $\prod_{m=4}^6(x_1+\gamma_m)=\gamma_4\gamma_5\gamma_6$
cancels with the prefactor, yielding a ratio whose every factor is nonzero:
$r_{1,-}^{(k)}>0$ since $\eta_{1,-}>0$;
$r_{2,-}^{(k)}>0$ since $k\eta_{2,+}<1$;
$(r_{2,-}^{(k)}-r_{1,-}^{(k)})\neq 0$ by~\eqref{eq:nondeg-bar};
the $\gamma_m$ are pairwise distinct by Assumption~\ref{ass:six-root}(ii);
and $r_{j,-}^{(k)}+\gamma_m\neq 0$ because $-r_{j,-}^{(k)}$ is a pole of $\psi_Z^{(k)}$,
so $\psi_Z^{(k)}(-r_{j,-}^{(k)})=\infty\neq q$, hence $\gamma_m\neq -r_{j,-}^{(k)}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:survival}]\label{proof:prop:survival}
\textit{Step 1: resolvent equation.}
Taking the Laplace transform of the PIDE $\partial_t p_{\mathrm{surv}} = \mathcal{L}^{(0)}p_{\mathrm{surv}}$
with initial condition $p_{\mathrm{surv}}(0,z;h_0)=1$ and killing condition $p_{\mathrm{surv}}=0$ for $z\le-h_0$,
exactly as in Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} with $g_k\equiv 1$, gives
\[
(q-\mathcal{L}^{(0)})\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,z;h_0)=1, \qquad z>-h_0.
\]

\textit{Step 2: particular solution.}
The constant forcing $f\equiv 1$ is an eigenfunction of $\mathcal{L}^{(0)}$ with eigenvalue
$\psi_Z^{(0)}(0)=0$, so the particular solution is
\[
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv,part}}(q,z) = \frac{1}{q-\psi_Z^{(0)}(0)} = \frac{1}{q}.
\]

\textit{Step 3: homogeneous solution.}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:Uhom} applied with $k=0$, the admissible homogeneous solution on $z>-h_0$ is
\[
\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv,hom}}(q,z;h_0)
=
C_1 e^{\gamma_4^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)}
+C_2 e^{\gamma_5^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)}
+C_3 e^{\gamma_6^{(0)}(q)(z+h_0)},
\]
where $\gamma_4^{(0)},\gamma_5^{(0)},\gamma_6^{(0)}$ are the roots of $\psi_Z^{(0)}(\gamma)=q$ with
$\Rea(\gamma)<0$.

\textit{Step 4: boundary conditions.}
The killing condition imposes, at $z=-h_0^+$:
\begin{enumerate}[(i)]
\item $\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q,-h_0^+;h_0)=0$;
\item $K_{j,-}^{(0)}[\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv}}](-h_0^+)=0$, \quad $j=1,2$,
\end{enumerate}
by the same argument as in Proposition~\ref{prop:Ubar}: downward jumps from $-h_0^+$ land in the killed region.

\textit{Step 5: linear system.}
Substituting the full solution $\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv}}=1/q+\widehat{U}_{\mathrm{surv,hom}}$ into each
condition and applying~\eqref{eq:phase-on-exp} to the homogeneous terms yields
\[
\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)\,\mathbf{C}(q;h_0) = -\begin{pmatrix}1/q\\1/q\\1/q\end{pmatrix},
\]
where the right-hand side entries are
$K_{j,-}^{(0)}[1/q](-h_0^+)=1/q$ (since $K_{j,-}^{(0)}$ integrates to one against the exponential density).
By Lemma~\ref{lem:Msurv-inv}, $\mathbf{M}_{\mathrm{surv}}(q)$ is invertible, so the coefficients $\mathbf{C}$ are uniquely determined.

\textit{Step 6: inversion and liquidation probability.}
Evaluating at $Z_0=0$ and inverting via the Bromwich formula gives $p_{\mathrm{surv}}(T,0;h_0)$. The
liquidation probability follows from the complement.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:Msurv-inv}]\label{proof:lem:Msurv-inv}
The argument is identical to that of Lemma~\ref{lem:Mbar-inv} with $k=0$.
The phase rates are $r_{1,-}^{(0)}=1/\eta_{1,-}$ and $r_{2,-}^{(0)}=1/\eta_{2,+}$,
so condition~\eqref{eq:nondeg-surv} is equivalent to $\eta_{1,-}\neq\eta_{2,+}$.
Each denominator factor is nonzero because $-r_{j,-}^{(0)}$ is a pole of $\psi_Z^{(0)}$,
hence cannot equal any root $\gamma_m^{(0)}(q)$ of $\psi_Z^{(0)}(\cdot)=q$ for finite $q$.
\end{proof}
